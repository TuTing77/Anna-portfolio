Reading

"The Old Man and the Sea"

"The Old Man and the Sea" is one of my favorite novels. It is a short story by the famous American writer Ernest Hemingway. He uses simple language to discuss the deeper meaning of life. After reading this book, I always reflect on our personal values and remain strong when facing life challenges.

This book is about an old fisherman named Santiago from Cuba. After 84 days, he caught a big fish. This fish dragged the boat for two days and two nights at sea. Then, suddenly, the old man encountered a shark. They fought to the death, but the big fish was still eaten by the shark. The old man only brought the fish bones home.

What impressed me the most was how Santiago fought against the shark. During the fight with the shark, the old man had many heartfelt conversations with himself. These conversations showed how he encouraged himself to overcome difficulties.

Although Santiago couldn't protect the fish, I think he ultimately did his best to protect his self-esteem. Facing long-term failures and loneliness, he didn't choose to give up but believed in himself and challenged the sea.

This story tells us that even if we fail, our efforts are still important. Santiago's never-give-up attitude, love for life, and excitement for new adventures gave us hope. Just like Santiago, we all face challenges in life. If we remain brave, full of hope, and not afraid of failure, we can find the meaning of life.

Speaking

Stars and Footprints

Two young people went hiking together. During the journey, they had a dispute over the route. One of them said some hurtful words in an overly aggressive manner. The injured young person felt very sad inside, but he just silently wrote on the snow:

"Today, my best friend hurt me with words."

The two continued to walk. The sky grew darker and the snowstorm suddenly intensified. They got lost and were cold and scared. Just at the critical moment, the young man who had said hurtful words found a sheltering cave and shared the only dry food with the other. After recovering his strength, the injured young man carved on the stone wall of the cave:

"Today, my best friend saved me and gave me warmth."

A friend asked him, "When I hurt you, you wrote it on the snow; but when I helped you, you carved it on the stone. Why is that?"

The young man replied: "Harm is like snow - it vanishes as soon as the wind blows; but kindness and assistance are like stars - they should always be engraved in our hearts, and will never be erased no matter how time passes."

Learn to write the hurts in places where they can easily fade away, and engrave kindness on the stones of life.

Dear Mr. Lee:

Thank you for visiting my blog. Time flies and this English course is coming to an end. This semester's language learning has given me a lot to gain: not only have I mastered the practical framework for writing emails, but I have also improved my oral expression through group discussions - in the "Cross-Cultural Body Language" topic, I was able to clearly express my opinions by combining classroom cases, which was something I was not good at before.

Looking back at my portfolio, my strength lies in having a relatively clear logical structure in written expression, but my fluency in spoken language still needs improvement. The most significant discovery I made this semester is that language learning is not about "memorizing grammar", but about "using scenarios" - accumulating vocabulary through daily conversations, which is more effective than rote memorization of words.

Looking ahead, my specific goals are: to achieve seamless daily communication with foreigners within 3 months, and to be able to independently complete a 200-word academic paragraph within 6 months. The action plan includes: 1. Spend 15 minutes each day doing "topic oral practice"; 2. Read a short article every week, extract 3 practical sentence patterns and rewrite them; 3. Complete 1 oral simulation dialogue every month.

Thank you, Professor, for your guidance this semester. I will continue to implement the plan and improve my language skills.

Sincerely,

Zheng Mei Ting (Anna)
